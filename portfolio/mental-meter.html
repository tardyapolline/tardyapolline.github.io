The Project

This Spring I studied Human-AI Design Methods at UC Berkeley. It was intimidating at first because it’s taught by the mechanical engineering / design innovation department and I have never taken a class in either domain. The class was structured around a single semester-long project. I teamed up with Elisa Lupin-Jimenez (Masters of Design), Madison Lee (BA in cognitive science), and Douglas McGowan (PhD in Mechanical Engineering) and we were mentored weekly by Rich Roth (senior UX designer from HP). Slowly, it started to click.

The core idea: The future of work is being shaped by automation and AI, but most tools prioritize speed over the human experience. People end up disconnected from meaning, agency, and each other. We focused on knowledge workers in hybrid environments like product managers, engineers, marketers who have to balance deep focus with constant responsiveness. Existing tools like Slack and email actually make things worse: more interruptions, weak support for context-switching, and zero help with burnout. Our goal was to imagine something better.

AI Expeditions

AI Expeditions

The course was built around three “AI Expeditions”, experiments to explore the potential and limitations of generative and analytical AI in the design process.

In Expedition 1, we studied the tension between deep focus and social connection. I spent time observing how physical layouts shape movement and behavior in the Design Institute Lab and Jacobs Hall. Then we interviewed people like Jorge, a hybrid worker who told me:

“We’re indoors, not close to the lights, in this cubicle the whole day. You can focus the whole time. So you have to force a stop—like going to the kitchen for water.”

He also said:

“It’s hard to approach someone. You have to knock on their cube and hope they don’t freak out because you scared them.”

That stuck with me. Meanwhile, another worker named Kahalley showed me the opposite strategy:

“Sometimes I just need a scenery change. If I’ve been in one room for a while, I’ll move to another room—or a coffee shop.”

My takeaway was that physical environments quietly shape whether we focus or connect. The best spaces do both, without forcing a choice.

My Imagined Solution

My Imagined Solution

I started thinking at the environmental level, considering architecture as the most passive, instinctive form of engagement. A well-designed space shouldn’t need signs or apps. It should invite focus or socialization through subtle, ambient cues: a warm shift in lighting, a change in visual privacy. I thought that environmental language could shape behavior without instruction.

Reading deeper on this topic, I came across “social interior design,” where workplace designers stimulate interaction by directing traffic and creating social destinations. I also loved the idea of “ambient displays”, information that lives at the periphery of your attention, like Hello.Wall, a wall-sized display designed for casual communication.

I found robotic furniture particularly exciting. One study used an Autonomous Mobile Robotic Partition that blocks acoustic and visual distractions in open offices. Surprisingly, people experienced the wall’s movement as a form of social interaction, a medium for indirect communication. Another 2025 study showed two robotic partitions that could nudge a distraction-causing coworker to relocate, almost gesturally.

I imagined a system of sliding glass walls that reconfigure to shape different spaces. The thinner the panels, the more fluid the walls could become. Each panel would use laminated smart glass that shifts slowly from transparent to opaque based on a worker’s availability. Transparency would allow for more spontaneous interaction, and opacity would facilitate focus. Embedded in the glass would be a transparent LED display showing natural scenes. A bright sunlit view would be energizing, and a warm glow would invite calm. This sits at the edge of what’s called “interactive adaptive architecture”: systems that adapt through interaction with people and their environment.

The Logo

The Logo

I developed the logo in Procreate and Illustrator. At first, I tried combining a brain and a lightning bolt to convey “Mental Meter” as mental energy. My teammate Madison experimented with typography using a single M for both words. Then we had a conversation that changed direction: what if the logo looked like our actual prototype? Our prototype was a battery. We made sketches until we realized the battery-level lines could form the letter M, like parallel bars. I experimented with colors, eventually settling on purple in different shades (matching the Mental Meter website). Then I added green streaks to create an illusion of 3D and “powering up.”

The Poster

The Poster

With the Mental Meter style and personality taking shape, Madison and I turned to the poster. We wanted someone to glance at it and instantly understand what it was about. We walked around a student showcase on the third floor to see what others had made. Most fell into two camps: dense academic posters (uninviting) or clean minimalist ones (better, but sometimes cold).

We decided to go airy and low-effort to read. This would reflect the battery’s philosophy: cognitive energy should be used consciously and balanced. Pinterest led us to Dyson posters and Apple minimalism. I used a Nikon to photograph the Mental Meter prototype next to my laptop on a wooden desk (to ground it in an office context). The battery sits center stage; the laptop faces slightly away because it’s meant to be peripheral. Photoshop helped me polish it, removing clutter like wires, adding the purple glow around the battery’s ring, and inserting the Mental Meter page with metrics.

Behance Website

Behance Website

We extended the same aesthetic into a Behance webpage using Figma. A purple gradient background continues the visual language from the photo. Rounded corners on title boxes and containers echo the battery’s soft edges. White text (like the battery itself) and purple shadows keep icons readable. We added a glass effect on some boxes for a modern feel, and simple icons helped us stay minimal and concise.

Reflections

Reflections

One of my favorite parts about this project was talking about Mental Meter with friends from other departments. A bioengineering friend helped me think about how cognitive energy and burnout could someday be measured more accurately in a non-obtrusive way. Right now we rely on self-reported energy, recovery logs, and activity trackers like ActivityWatch. But future work could use thermal cameras to read facial stress signals in real time. The ultimate question is how we can balance minimal effort from the workers while preserving their privacy.
