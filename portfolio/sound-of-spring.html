The Sound of Spring

For the closing assignment of Professor Senem Pirlerâ€™s Sound Design for Moving Images course, I chose to conclude my studies with a bold experiment: a silent film. After months of experimenting with sound design: foley art, sound effects editing, post-processing and sound mixing, field recording techniques, and soundscaping,... I felt a void, one that could only be filled by the perspective of a deaf student. And so began my journey into the ways sound can be experienced without hearing, leading to the creation of a film that may be silent to the ear but rich and resonant to the remaining senses.

Senem had given us many exercises during the semester, my favorite being field recording. It was like a treasure hunt, with every sound a buried treasure only waiting to be unearthed with patience and careful listening, and the Bennington College campus a vast map full of sonic riches. For the final assignment, I chose not a microphone, but a camera as my instrument. The winters in Vermont are relentless, their icy grip lingering long after the calendar says it should end. But when I raised my camera, the thaw began, revealing the earth's first brushstrokes of life and color. Enchanted by the moment, I started recording The Sound of Spring.
